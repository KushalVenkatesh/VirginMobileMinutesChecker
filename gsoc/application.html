  1. Describe your organization.

    We are a group of developers who have worked together in the past and maintain an open-source Android app for pre-paid cell phone users to track the number of minutes they use per month. The app has over 30,000 users, many community patches, and a continuous stream of email feedback and Market ratings from users.

  2. Why is your organization applying to participate in GSoC? What do you hope to gain by participating?

    We were motivated to apply for the opportunity to mentor students, and in that light we have two specific objecties that we'd like to meet:

    1. Teach students software engineering skills beyond classroom instruction.

    It is atypcial for an engineering curriculum to have students to look at a codebase with many contributors. It is not often that students get to make changes to software and receive real, live feedback from users the moment changes go live. How do you deal with the inevitable "one-star" review? How do you communicate with remote teams?

    These scenarios exemplify skills which are core to the profession of software development, and are, at best, only simulated in the classroom.

    We want students to build something that thousands of people will use and feel ownership of their results, rather than a one-off course project.

    2. Improve our own community process and gain experience mentoring others. As engineers, one of our responsibilities is to mentor others - be it interns, new team members, or trying to explain software to those crazy biz guys! GSoC gives us a chance to hone those skills. GSoC is part of our own professional development.

    Notably absent from our goals are pushing out new features! Of course we want to improve our product and cross things off the list that we have not yet gotten to, but that will be a side-effect of good mentoring and of students learning about the practical side of software.

  9. Criteria for Mentors

    The mentors are people who I have personally worked with in the past on sundry projects. They always follow through and keep commitments, and have strong technical and communication skills. All mentors are proficient in Java, and some mentors have a lot of mobile UX design experience.

  10. Disappearing Students?

    This project should be treated with the same urgency as a full-time job; we have a product that we must support and maintain, so we will keep track of progress and have communication on a daily basis - just as we do in the office.

    If students are not able to meet our original goals, but are communicative, then that is fine; this is a learning experience and we don't expect things to go perfectly.

    We will talk with students if things are slipping - a telltale sign will be that we do not hear from them for a while. If a student completely falls off the radar, and we're not able to understand their situation to be flexible, we will have to ask the student to leave.

  11. Disappearing Mentors?

    As with students, mentors are expected to be available at a schedule that we have previously agreed upon. We have a pool of several mentors, so if one is not able to continue then we have others who can step in. Mentors will be working with each other and keeping track of each other's progress. If a mentor is unable to perform their duties then someone else will be chosen.

  12. What steps will you take to encourage students to interact with your project's community before, during and after the program?

    We want students to immediately start looking at code, running it on their own machines, and seeing what kinds of projects they would be interested in. We will specifically elicit this kind of feedback as they consider working with us and come up with a project that would be useful and that the student would be interested in.

    We realize that students may not have Virgin Mobile accounts or devices for development; this is okay, we are happy to provide devices for them for the duration of this project.

    During the program we will have regular checkpoints, code reviews, and hopefully we will push out plenty of updates to get feedback from users.

    After the summer, it would be great if students were still interested in this app and its impact; possibly they could be mentors next year!
